Business Context & Stakes

Inventory decisions with real operational cost

Seed producers face a classic supply-chain tradeoff: overstock ties up working capital and warehouse space; understock means missed planting windows and lost dealer orders. Capacity is finite—every SKU prioritized is another delayed.

SKU prioritization

Which product lines (PLFs) deserve scarce production and warehouse slots when demand is uneven across years and traits?

Reorder risk

Volatile or declining SKUs create stockout exposure. Stable, high-volume lines need reliable reorder cadence—not reactive firefighting.

Capacity constraints

Planners need evidence—not gut feel—to allocate limited acreage, processing, and inventory dollars across hundreds of seed variants.

Data Pipeline

From production-line CSVs to analysis-ready frames

An ETL workflow ingests multi-year, wide-format production exports, normalizes schema drift across seasons, and joins quantity, trait, company, and yield attributes for forecasting and BI.

01

Ingest

Load messy, year-sliced production-line CSVs with inconsistent column layouts and naming.

02

Transform

Normalize to long-form records—PLF, year, trait, company, quantity—ready for aggregation and modeling.

03

Enrich

Merge yield and maturity fields; compute stability (CV), trend slopes, and reorder-risk features by SKU cluster.

04

Serve

Publish filtered views and model outputs into an interactive dashboard for weekly planning reviews.

Methodology

Predictive reorder-risk & demand forecasting

Models estimate future quantity by PLF cluster, then surface stability and trend signals that planners use to flag reorder risk under capacity limits.

Demand forecasting

Regression and ML candidates are scored on holdout R² and RMSE to select the best predictor of next-season quantity by PLF bin.

Reorder-risk signals

Coefficient of variation and multi-year slope highlight volatile or declining SKUs that need tighter inventory buffers—or deliberate deprioritization.

SKU clustering

Elbow-guided PLF binning groups similar demand profiles so forecasts and planning rules scale beyond one-off SKU analysis.
